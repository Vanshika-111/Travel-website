Corsica

where mountains meet the sea, and every sunset whispers a story of wild beauty.

About

Corsica is a beautiful Mediterranean island belonging to France, located southeast of the French mainland and just
north of Sardinia (Italy). Known for its dramatic coastal scenery, rugged mountains, and rich cultural identity, Corsica
offers a unique blend of French and Italian influences.

History:


Ancient Roots

Inhabited since 9000 BC, Corsica has a rich prehistoric legacy with stone structures and archaeological sites.
It was settled by Greeks, then ruled by Carthaginians and later the Romans (from 240 BC), who developed roads and cities.


Medieval Era

After the fall of the Roman Empire, Corsica faced invasions from Vandals, Moors, and Byzantines.
Eventually, control passed to the Republic of Pisa and then the Genoese in the 13th century.

Short-Lived Independence

In 1755, Pasquale Paoli, a Corsican patriot, declared the island a republic, writing a democratic constitution.
This early democracy influenced even the U.S. Constitution.
